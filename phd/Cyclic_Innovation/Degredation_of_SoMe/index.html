Cycles of Digital Community: A Sociotechnical Model of the Rise and Fall of Social Platforms from IRC to TikTok

Abstract

This paper proposes a sociotechnical lifecycle model for online social platforms, consisting of seven recurring stages: Inception, Early Adoption, Mass Appeal, Commercialization, Stagnation, Migration, and Abandonment. Drawing on case studies from IRC, Reddit, Facebook, and Twitter, as well as theoretical frameworks in media ecology, platform capitalism, and digital sociology, we argue that the collapse of online communities stems not only from economic and technical pressures but from social dynamics—especially the effects of anonymity, identity rigidity, and exclusionary cultural behavior. The persistence of these patterns across decades suggests a structural phenomenon worthy of deeper academic inquiry.

1. Introduction

  • Hypothesis: Social platforms follow a predictable lifecycle that can be observed historically and structurally.
  • Overview of prior scholarship:
  • danah boyd (social network sites, teen engagement)
  • José van Dijck (cultural history of platforms)
  • Nick Srnicek (platform capitalism)
  • Introduce the sociotechnical lifecycle: 7 stages.

2. Historical Foundations: IRC as Proto-Social Media

  • IRC origins (1988), cultural epicenter for early digital sociality.
  • Early adoption: niche, tech-heavy, identity-masked.
  • Migration and commercialization at odds: e.g. Freenode becomes a support hub, but staffed by impatient, often exclusionary tech culture.
  • Decline due to rigidity, user hostility, and inability to evolve UX.
  • Source examples:
  • Reid, "Electropolis" (1991)
  • Hafner, Where Wizards Stay Up Late (1996)

3. Lifecycle Model

The 7-Stage Sociotechnical Lifecycle:

  1. Inception
  2. Ideological, experimental, limited audience.

  3. IRC, LiveJournal, early Twitter.

  4. Early Adoption

  5. Tight-knit, culture-defining communities form.

  6. High trust, low friction.

  7. Mass Appeal

  8. Exponential growth; media attention.

  9. Shift from community -> audience.

  10. Commercialization

  11. Ads, influencer culture, venture capital pressure.

  12. Algorithmic feeds, data harvesting.

  13. Stagnation

  14. UX declines, feature bloat, institutional rigidity.

  15. Community splits and tensions grow.

  16. Migration

  17. Power users depart; parallel ecosystems rise.

  18. Examples: Discord, Mastodon, Substack.

  19. Abandonment / Ghost Town

  20. Bots, spam, diehards remain.
  21. MySpace, Google+, Vine.

4. Social Decay: Hostility, Rigidity, and the Toxic Core

  • Online Disinhibition Effect (Suler, 2004): Anonymity unleashes toxic behavior.
  • Cultural Lock-in: Users resist changes; identity becomes tied to outdated systems.
  • IRC as case study:
  • Freenode's degeneration due to support-hostility.
  • Example of "support paradox"—most technical users are least empathetic.
  • Disability and neurodivergence:
  • Platforms act as social prosthetics for users with limited offline engagement.
  • These users resist migration and become guardians of legacy culture.
  • Ethical framing:
  • Use "neurodivergent," avoid pathologizing.
  • Cite insiders where possible.

5. Causal Mechanisms

  • Economic incentives: Attention economy and surveillance capitalism (Srnicek).
  • Governance and moderation failure (Gillespie).
  • Fragmentation and identity collapse.
  • Psychological/social fatigue: social media as a performance.

6. Resilience and Exceptions?

  • Can any platform escape the cycle?
  • Federation, decentralization (Mastodon, Matrix).
  • Co-op and Web3 attempts.
  • Challenges: UX, discoverability, fragmentation.
  • Nostalgia as a trap: early adopters often rewrite history.
  • Suggest need for sustainable governance and community ethics.

7. Conclusion

  • Lifecycle is a pattern, not a destiny—but few platforms course-correct.
  • Suggest future work:
  • Longitudinal studies of user migration.
  • Neurodivergent experience in digital communities.
  • Comparative analysis of federated vs centralized platforms.
